Grounds of Inadmissibility and deportability

Grounds of Inadmissibility
...

grounds of inadmissibility apply to condition/conduct/criminal conviction that can cause a non-citizen to be unable to get some new lawful immigration status or other benefit

  • APPLIES TO
    • applicants for adjustment of status
    • people seeking admission at the border
    • LPR who falls into INA §101(a)(13)(c):
      1. has abandoned or relinquished that status,
      2. has been absent from the United States for a continuous period in excess of 180 days,
      3. has engaged in illegal activity after having departed the United States,
      4. has departed from the United States while under legal process seeking removal of the alien from the United States, including removal proceedings under this Act and extradition proceedings,
      5. has committed a Crime Involving Moral Turpitude, unless §212(h) applies, or
      6. has not been admitted to the United States after inspection and authorization by an immigration officer.
        • A noncitizen who gains admission to the United States by pretending to be a U.S. citizen or LPR has not yet been "admitted"
  • DOES NOT APPLY TO
    • LPR convicted of CIMT and placed into removal proceedings
    • LPR who returns to the US from vacation
  • The government has the burden of proof that person is inadmissible
    • the applicant has the burden of proof to prove that they are clearly and beyond doubt entitled to be admitted and not inadmissible
      • lower than beyond reasonable doubt.
Burden of Proof for Inadmissibility
...
  • Burden of Proof for non-citizens
    • noncitizens who are subject to the grounds of inadmissibility, bear the burden of proving either:
      1. that they are “unequivocally clearly and beyond doubt entitled to be admitted and not inadmissible under section 212 or,
      2. by clear and convincing evidence, that they are lawfully present in the United States pursuant to a prior admission
  • Burden of proof for LPR
    • LPRs who are subject to the grounds of inadmissibility as 'arriving aliens' (ie. returning from a trip abroad) have more rights than other non-citizens
      • they are entitled to due process protections
    • Gov has the burden of proof

Crimes Involving Moral Turpitude
...

INA §212(a)(2)

  • what is a Crime Involving Moral Turpitude?

    • decided by case law
      • a crime involves “moral turpitude” if it is reprehensible conduct with a culpable mental state
        • mens rea is at least recklessness
        • the potential injury is serious bodily injury or death
        • aggravating factors if the victim is a a person whom society views as deserving of special protection (ex. child/spouse/peace-officer/elderly)
    • fraud and theft with intent to permanently deprive are always CIMTs

  • There must be either an admission or conviction of the crime

    • the admission must involve sufficient facts
      • if the applicant enrolled in a diversion program criminal grounds of inadmissibility does not apply
    • if another court has found the applicant guilty, BIA will refer to that conviction
    • Limited impact of post conviction relief
      • "state-court orders for post-conviction relief will be given effect only when the orders are based on a procedural or substantive defect in the underlying criminal proceeding." Matter of Thomas and Thompson
        • ex. of post conviction relief: vacate conviction, modifies conviction, clarifies conviction
exceptions to CIMT
...
youthful offender
...
  • three requirements
    1. committed only one CIMT
    2. committed the offense below age 18
    3. conviction/imprisonment occurred at least 5 years before the application
  • A disposition in juvenile delinquency proceedings is not a “conviction” of a crime for immigration purposes
  • an admission made by a minor, or by an adult about a CIMT committed while a minor, does not trigger inadmissibility
petty offense
...
  • does not apply to deportation proceedings
  • three requirements:
    1. committed only one CIMT ever
    2. sentence was less than 6mo
    3. the max penalty was less than 1 year
  • possession of 30g of marijuana or less
  • INA §212(h) - doesn't apply to those who entered EWI
When only a part of the criminal statue involves a CIMT
...
    1. the statue was found to be over-broad
    1. the statue has to be found to be divisible
    • the statue must include two distinct crimes.
    • factors to consider for divisibility:
      • The statue is written in the disjunctive (used or a lot in the statue)
      • jury instructions traditionally differ between the two crimes
        • ie. the prosecutor must prove different elements for the two crimes
      • The statue has two separate sentencing requirements
    1. One of the crimes is a CIMT and the other is not.
    1. The applicant is only inadmissible if they were found guilty of the CIMT section of the statue
categorical approach
...
  • it is necessary to use categorical analysis bc the text of the INA is vague and there are practical difficulties and potential unfairness under a factual approach Steps of the categorical approach:
  1. find the generic offense
    • the generic offense is what the INA list
  2. compare the generic offense to the statue of conviction
    • does the offense defined in the INA similar to the statue that the individual was convicted under
    • If the INA does not define the offense, courts look at:
      • dictionary definitions
      • common characteristics of state and federal statutes
  3. if the statue of the conviction is over broad, determine if divisible
    • divisible = where one part of the statue would match the generic offense and another part does not
      • ex. enhanced punishments
  4. if it is divisible, look at the record to determine whether the individual convicted of generic offense
  5. if it is not divisible, done and not a match
modified categorical approach
...
  • modified categorical approach merely assists the sentencing court in identifying the defendant's crime of conviction
    • the modified categorical approach does not apply to statutes that contain a single, “indivisible” set of elements sweeping more broadly than the corresponding generic offense.
  • modified categorical approach merely asks not whether “statutory definitions” necessarily require an adjudicator to find the generic offense, but instead whether the prosecutor's case realistically led the adjudicator to make that determination
    • the approach does not authorize the court to try to discern what a trial showed, or a plea proceeding revealed, about the defendant's underlying conduct

Non Criminal Grounds of Inadmissibility
...

  • Deportation or Subsequent Reentry or Attempted Reentry Without Inspection
    • INA §212(a)(9)(C)
    • Permanent bar for prior deportation or subsequent reentry or attempted reentry without inspection.
      • even getting approved from Consular Process for this is unlikely.
  • Unlawful presence
    • INA §212(a)(9)(B)
    • Prior unlawful presence after April 1, 1997. Waiver is available.
    • IMPORTANT: unlawful presence is only triggered by a departure
      • if someone has been living in the United States and has never left since their initial entry, they will not have to worry about unlawful presence
  • Fraud and Misrepresentation
    • INA §212(a)(6)(C)(i)
    • Waiver is available for extreme hardship
  • Alien smuggling
    • INA §212(a)(6)(E) "any person who at any time knowingly has encouraged, induced, assisted, abetted, or aided any other alien to enter...in violation of law is inadmissible.”
    • Waiver is available if the person:
      • (1) smuggled only their parent, spouse, sons or daughter and
      • (2) is adjusting based on family petition that is not a f4 petition
  • Public Charge
    • INA § 212(a)(4), "a person who, “in the opinion of the Attorney General at the time of application for admission or adjustment of status, is likely to be a public charge is inadmissible.”
    • Must file an I-864 (affidavit of support) for family-based petitions

Grounds of Deportability
...

The following people are subject to the grounds of deportability:

  • Nonimmigrant visa holders within the United States following a lawful admission;
  • People admitted as visa waiver entrants
  • Visa holder and visa waiver overstays in the United States
  • Refugees
  • Lawful permanent residents that do not fall under 101(a)(13)(c)
Burden of Proof for Deportability
...
  • the government bears the burden of proving, by clear and convincing evidence, that the noncitizen is deportable
  • For criminal grounds of deportability, the government bears the burden of proving both
    • (1) the existence of a criminal conviction; and
    • (2) that the conviction triggers a ground of deportability
  • Non-citizen
    • where a noncitizen is subject to the deportability grounds, the government must submit clear and convincing, credible proof of deportability, which the noncitizen then has the burden of rebutting, before the noncitizen’s silence can be used against them

Criminal Grounds of Deportability
...

  • convicted of an aggravated felony
    • INA §101(a)(43) (aggravated felony) provides the definition of an aggravated felony
    • In most federal courts, a conviction for any offense listed as an “aggravated felony” is grounds for deportation, even if the crime was not considered an “aggravated felony” at the time of conviction.
  • Crime of Domestic Violence
    • INA §237(a)(2)(E) Crimes of domestic violence
    • The definition of crime of violence for a crime of domestic violence is the same as for aggravated felonies. However, there is not a requirement of a one- year sentence here. Second, the offense must be against a current or former spouse or partner
  • convicted of a CIMT
    • INA §237(a)(2)(A) General crimes
    • 1 CIMT within 5 years of admission with max sentencing of 1 year
      • admission = date of physical entrance into the united states
        • except for those arriving EWI
        • except for LPR re-entering the US who fall under 101(a)(13)(C)
    • 2 or more CIMTs arising from different criminal schemes
  • any controlled substance conviction
    • INA §237(a)(2)(B) Controlled substances
    • exception for single offense of simple possession of 30 grams or less that occurred before 2011
